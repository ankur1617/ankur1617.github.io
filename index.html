Ankur Biswas

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Integrative Biology at Oklahoma State University, with research interests spanning aquatic ecology, environmental toxicology, and molecular biology. My work focuses on harmful algal blooms (HABs) and the environmental drivers that regulate the production of cyanobacterial toxins.

More broadly, I investigate how environmental conditions shape the production and activity of bioactive molecules, and how these compounds influence biological systems across scales. Cyanobacterial toxins such as microcystins are classically studied as environmental hazards, yet they are also highly specific modulators of cellular pathways, particularly through inhibition of serine/threonine protein phosphatases (e.g., PP1 and PP2A). This dual identity positions them at the intersection of environmental toxicology and therapeutic toxicology.

My research is motivated by the idea that understanding how environmental factors regulate toxin biosynthesis can inform not only risk assessment and ecosystem management, but also the broader study of bioactive natural products with pharmacological potential. By linking environmental drivers to molecular function, I aim to contribute to a framework where naturally occurring toxins are viewed not only as contaminants, but also as tools for probing cellular signaling and as leads for therapeutic exploration.

Ultimately, my goal is to develop integrative and predictive approaches that connect environmental change to toxin production, biological activity, and human health outcomes. This includes advancing our ability to anticipate toxic events in freshwater systems while also contributing to the mechanistic understanding of compounds that sit at the boundary of toxicity and therapeutic utility.
